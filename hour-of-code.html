Team 33

How software works? Hour of Code

The main purpose of the event, which took place at School No. 229(Baku) was to introduce the 29 young minds to the world of programming. By the end of the lesson, pupils were able to understand basic algorithmic actions, block-based coding, debugging and were able to work with simple animations. With the help of slides, the session has started with Natig displaying some brief information about the ADA University – we covered important aspects such as the amount of different carrier choices, the language of study, and the bright future of an ADA student. Specifically, we discussed the School of IT and Engineering. After being done with the presentation, we set up the environment for the event. The team also informed the pupils that the most active of them, will be rewarded. The purpose of this was promote healthy competition among students and boost their motivation. As the first 5 problems of the Classic Maze puzzle were a breeze, which covered the most basic block-based coding actions such as moving forward, turning left and right, students have quickly moved on to the next puzzles. These puzzles are where the first struggles have come up, as students were now required not only to connect their sprite to the target, to complete the puzzle, but also to find the easiest and shortest way to do so. The young minds were now informed about loops, in our case, it is called the repetition block. Although puzzles 6, 7 and 8 were not as complicated, they took longer to be done with. While Natig was explaining and elaborating on the 6th puzzle by searching for some volunteers, Masud, Maharram and Fidan went on to help pupils that quickly got to the 9th question, which was one of the most troubling ones. The common issue among the students on this puzzle was understanding which commands are supposed to be in the repeat block and which are not. Through interactive explanations, we managed to enlighten the pupils. Later on, students were introduced to the new type of block – the repeat until-do loop block. While similar to the repeat block, it is distinct – since it does not require to specify the number of times the loop must be executed. The repeat until-do block is to be executed as long as the sprite has not met the target. Hence, students spotted the similarities and did not need further explanation when working on puzzles 10 and 11. Puzzle 14 was another struggling point of the students, – even though it consists of commands that our team has explained to the pupils earlier, it might take some thinking time to find the correct algorithm, considering that there is a new type of block command – if statements. With a little bit of guidance from all team members, students managed to solve the puzzle the following way: first, we encouraged them to build an algorithm with no use of the loop blocks; after recognizing the pattern, they have successfully placed the loops. To consolidate this information, Natig asked for a volunteer to demonstrate puzzle 15 on the board and elaborate on the thought process. Out of many students, three came up to the whiteboard and solved the puzzle as a team, while demonstrating it to those who struggled with this problem. They have noticed that the sprite, in this case, must not turn left, only right, which meant that the sprite should be moving forward if there is no path to the right. As the students progressed to the final 5 puzzles, the class had its top three finalists. They were awarded notebooks for their problem-solving skills and speed. On the 18th puzzle, students faced an extended version of the if loop – if-else loop. However, this was more than easy to deal with for them. They quickly realised which commands must be placed in empty spaces. To ensure that all the pupils understood the idea of this extended loop, team members have once again searched for volunteers to help others clarify their knowledge of the block. Our puzzle of choice was the 19th question. The chosen participants no longer needed as much help from the team members as they did at the beginning of the lesson – showcasing the result of successful topic coverage. Although the rest of the students fundamentally understood the basics of algorithmic thinking – especially that the code is read by the compiler from top to bottom, some had trouble with the 20th question. Since it was the most advanced topic, nested ifs, many of the pupils struggled for with it. At first, they were not able to understand, which commands and/or blocks must be nested. However, after with the help of the collaborative approach, the whole class, as a team, managed to quickly find the solution to the issue – they realised that the nested “if” must be in the “else’s” empty space of the first If loop. After confirming that each student had successfully completed the Maze Puzzle with understanding, team members awarded each student a sticker pack to recognize their determination and hard work. Following this, we held a certification ceremony and concluded the event by expressing our gratitude to all the students, the school administration, and ADA University. We also want to note that, as Team 33, we made every effort to ensure that each student fully engaged with the lesson and stayed motivated throughout. As a result, the event lasted for 1 hour and 30 minutes.
